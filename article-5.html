Segmenting Networks with VLANs

Discover how Virtual LANs can dramatically improve your network's security and performance by logically isolating traffic.

By Zakaria Rami July 24, 2025

Key Takeaways

  • VLANs (Virtual LANs) logically segment a physical network into multiple, isolated broadcast domains.
  • They enhance security by preventing devices in one VLAN from communicating with another unless explicitly allowed by a router.
  • VLANs improve performance by containing broadcast traffic within a single VLAN, reducing unnecessary network chatter.
  • The IEEE 802.1Q standard is the protocol used to tag traffic with VLAN information across switches.

The Problem with Flat Networks

Imagine a small office with all devices connected to a single switch. This is known as a "flat network." While simple, this design has major drawbacks as the network grows. Every broadcast message (like a DHCP request) is sent to every single device, creating unnecessary traffic and slowing down the network. More importantly, every device can, by default, see every other device, posing a significant security risk. A compromised guest device could potentially access sensitive company servers.

What is a VLAN?

A Virtual Local Area Network (VLAN) is the solution. A VLAN allows you to group devices together into a logical network, regardless of their physical location. Devices within a VLAN can communicate with each other as if they were on their own private switch, but they are isolated from devices in other VLANs.

For example, you could create:

  • VLAN 10: For the Engineering department.
  • VLAN 20: For the Sales department.
  • VLAN 30: For guest wireless access.

Even if an engineer and a salesperson are plugged into the same physical switch, their devices would be in separate, isolated networks.

How VLANs Work: The 802.1Q Tag

For VLANs to work across multiple switches, the switches need a way to identify which VLAN a piece of traffic belongs to. This is accomplished using the IEEE 802.1Q standard. When a data frame leaves a switch port that is part of a VLAN, the switch inserts a small "tag" into the frame's header. This tag contains the VLAN ID (e.g., 10, 20, or 30).

When the next switch receives the frame, it reads the tag and knows which VLAN the traffic belongs to, ensuring it only forwards the frame to other ports in that same VLAN.

Conclusion

VLANs are a fundamental technology for any modern network administrator. They provide a powerful and flexible way to segment your network, dramatically improving both security and performance. By logically grouping users and devices, you can create a more organized, efficient, and resilient network infrastructure.
